\hypertarget{part-d}{%
\paragraph{Part (D)}\label{part-d}}

By ``fitted line'', my understanding is that this is the line of
\(\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X\hat{\beta}}\), in which \(\mathbf{y}\) is the
dependent variable country's GDP growth rate (GR6096), \(\mathbf{X}\) is
the independent variable the country's level of defense spending as a
fraction of its GDP (DEF60), and \(\mathbf{\hat{\beta}}\) is the mean of
\(\mathbf{\beta}\)'s posterior distribution -- note that this mean term
is the same for conditional posterior and marginal posterior, both of
which doesn't have the term \(\omega\). We have such mean term as
\[\mathbf{\hat{\beta}} = \mathbf{U^{-1}V^T}\] in which
\(\mathbf{U}=\mathbf{X^T\Lambda X+K}\),
\(\mathbf{V}=\mathbf{y^T\Lambda X+m^TK}\).

As the problem suggested, we set
\(\mathbf{\Lambda}=\mathbf{I}_{2\times 2}\), and
\(\mathbf{K}=\text{diag}(K_1,K_2)\) be something vague -- I pick
\(K_1 = K_2 = 0.1\). Moreover, I pick the prior mean of
\(\mathbf{\beta}\) as
\(\mathbf{m}=\begin{bmatrix}0.1, 0.1\end{bmatrix}^T\) -- with the
precision so vague, this prior mean term won't have too much effect on
posterior parameter \(\mathbf{\hat{\beta}}\).
